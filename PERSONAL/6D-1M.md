## Overview

6D+1M is a tiny framework to filter statements fast, without turning your brain into a full-time fact-checking department.

It answers one question:

Can I treat this claim as **actionable**, **tentative**, or **noise**?

### Quick Takeaways

1. **Definition** — If terms are vague, the claim is vague
2. **Data** — No observable anchor, no confidence
3. **Design** — Correlation isn’t causation; design limits conclusions
4. **Distortions** — Confounds and biases create fake “truth”
5. **Degree** — Effect size matters more than vibes
6. **Mind-change** — Define what evidence would update your belief

## The Framework

### 1) Definition

Goal: make the claim _operational_, not poetic.

- What does the key term mean **exactly**?
- Who/where/when does it apply? (population, context, horizon)

### 2) Data

Goal: locate the observable anchor.

- Is there **measurement** (numbers/records), or only assertion?
- Primary source or hearsay? Is the method transparent?

### 3) Design (Study / Evidence Design)

Goal: know what kind of inference is allowed.

- RCT / natural experiment / longitudinal / cross-sectional / case study?
- Is the claim **correlational** or **causal**?

### 4) Distortions (Confounds / Bias)

Goal: name the 1–2 strongest illusion generators.

- What likely confounds could explain it? (SES, selection bias, reverse causality)
- Were they controlled, discussed, or tested for robustness?

### 5) Degree (Effect Size / Practical Impact)

Goal: separate “real” from “technically significant.”

- How big is the effect? (effect size, RR/OR, points, %, practical change)
- Is it **material** in real life, or only a p-value trophy?

### +1) Mind-change (What Would Update My Mind)

Goal: avoid identity-based beliefs.

- What evidence would make you revise? (meta-analysis, prereg replication, better design, stronger data)
- What result would falsify the claim?

## 30-Second Flow

Ask these in order:

1. **Definition**: do I understand the claim in measurable terms?
2. **Data**: what observation backs it?
3. **Design**: what inference is allowed (cause vs association)?
4. **Distortions**: what confound/bias could fake it?
5. **Degree**: is it big enough to matter?
6. **Mind-change**: what would change my mind?

Output: **Actionable / Tentative / Noise**

- **Actionable**: terms clear, evidence decent, distortion addressed, magnitude meaningful
- **Tentative**: partial evidence, unclear magnitude, distortions plausible
- **Noise**: vague terms + no data + unsupported causal leap

---

## Rubric (Fast Scoring)

Score each axis 0–2.

- **0**: missing / vague / not addressed
- **1**: partial / weak / unclear
- **2**: clear / strong / directly addressed

Suggested interpretation:

- **9–12**: actionable (with normal caution)
- **5–8**: tentative (ok to test with low-risk experiments)
- **0–4**: noise (ignore or ask for better grounding)

---

## One-Line Template

Use this as the default output (copy-paste friendly):

> This claim is **[well-defined / vague]**, grounded in **[data + design]**, vulnerable to **[main distortion]**, the effect is **[small / medium / large]**, and I’d update if **[evidence X]**.

## Examples

### Example A — “Cold shower makes you disciplined.”

- **Definition**: “disciplined” = what behavior, measured how?
- **Data**: anecdotes vs tracked adherence metrics
- **Design**: correlation (people who already train hard do cold showers) vs intervention
- **Distortions**: selection bias, novelty effect
- **Degree**: does it improve adherence meaningfully, or just mood for 30 minutes?
- **Mind-change**: “If a well-run RCT shows no adherence change vs control, I downgrade belief.”

Verdict: usually **tentative** → test it low-risk with tracking.

### Example B — “X causes Y.”

Instant checks:

- If the source is cross-sectional, treat “causes” as **overclaim**.
- If there’s no counterfactual design, ask for **mechanism + robustness**.

Verdict: **tentative/noise** until design supports causality.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Paper worship**: “No paper = false.” Wrong. It may be unstudied or context-specific.
- **Vibe worship**: “Feels right = true.” Also wrong.
- **Significance trophy**: p-value without magnitude/practical impact.
- **Survivorship bias**: you only see winners posting outcomes.
- **Reverse causality**: Y causes X, not the other way.

---

## When to Escalate

Use the 30-second check first. Escalate to deeper research when:

- Stakes are high (health, money, irreversible decisions)
- You’re about to build a habit/system around the claim
- You see strong incentives for the source to mislead
- The claim is being used to justify a policy or moral judgement
